Buffers

1. Strong Acids

Strong acids, such as hydrochloric acid, exist almost completely as ions in water. For clarity, water molecules are not shown in this animation.

2. Weak Acids

Weak acids, such as hydrofluoric acid, exist as molecules and ions in water.

3. Le Chatelier's principle

Le Chatelier's principle applies to chemical systems that are in equilibrium. It is used to predict what will happen to a reversible chemical reaction when conditions are changed. The principle states that if temperature, pressure or concentration of reactants in a reversible chemical reaction are changed, the equilibrium will shift so as to minimise any change.

For example, consider this reversible reaction:

A+B↔C+D

If the concentration of C is increased, then the equilibrium shifts to the left, minimising the increase in concentration of C.

If the concentration of C is decreased, then the equilibrium shifts to the right, minimising the decrease in concentration of C.

4. Strong and Weak acids

How does a buffer work?

Select tabs Steps 1 to Step 5 below to see what happens when a strong acid (HCl) is added to an aqueous solution of a weak acid (HF).

The weak acid (HF) is only slightly ionised, so there is a low concentration of hydrogen and fluoride ions present at equilibrium.

A small amount of hydrochloric acid is added to the hydrofluoric acid solution. Hydrochloric acid is a strong acid that almost completely ionises in water.

Adding hydrochloric acid increases the concentration of hydrogen ions in solution. The solution becomes more acidic and its pH decreases.

Le Chatelier's principle predicts that an increase in hydrogen ion concentration will drive the equilibrium reaction HF↔H++F- to the left. Some free fluoride ions will combine with hydrogen ions to form HF. The hydrogen ion concentration decreases, the solution becomes less acidic and the pH increases.

Adding HCl has increased the pH, but only by a small amount. A new equilibirum has been established, but almost all of the free fluroide ions have been used up. Chloride ions play no part in the equilibirum, hence their name 'spectator ions'.

6. Effective buffers

Hydrofluoric acid is not a very effective buffer because there aren't many fluoride ions available in solution that can combine with added hydrogen ions.

To make a more effective buffer, the concentration of available fluoride ions must be increased. Sodium fluoride is added to the hydrofluoric acid solution to do this.

Sodium ions are spectator ions in the buffering process, but the increased concentration of fluoride ions makes the solution a much more effective buffer.

Sodium fluoride is an ionic salt that contains the conjugate base of the weak acid, hydrofluoric acid.

7. An effective buffers

How does a mixture of hydrofluoric acid and sodium fluoride work as a buffer?

Select tabs Steps 1 to Step 5 below to see what happens when a strong acid (HCl) is added to a mixture of a weak acid (HF) and an ionic salt containing its conjugate base (NaF).

The buffer contains a high concentration of fluoride ions because sodium fluoride completely dissociates in aqueous solution. As before, only a small proportion of hydrofluoric acid ionises.

A small amount of hydrochloric acid is added to the buffer solution. Hydrochloric acid is a strong acid that almost completely ionises in water.

Adding hydrochloric acid to the buffer increases the concentration of hydrogen ions. The solution is now more acidic and the pH lower.

Using Le Chatelier's principle, an increase in hydrogen ion concentration drives the equilibrium reaction HF↔H++F- to the left. Some of the free fluoride ions combine with hydrogen ions to form HF. The hydrogen ion concentration decreases, the solution becomes less acidic and the pH increases.

Adding HCl has not affected the pH. A new equilibirum has been established by combining some free fluoride ions with hydrogen ions. If more hydrochloric acid is added to the buffer, there are still plenty of fluoride ions left in solution that can combine with hydrogen ions.

8. Summary

Buffers resist changes in pH.

An effective buffer consists of a weak acid and a salt containing its conjugate base.

Le Chatelier's Principle cna be used to explain how a buffer works.

An increase in hydrogen ions is resisted by combining hydrogen ions with the conjugate base. This makes more molecules of the weak acid.

A decrease in hydrogen ions is resisted by increased dissociation of the weak acid.

Buffers are all around us: in our bodies and in the environment. They maintain stable pH conditions necessary for life.
