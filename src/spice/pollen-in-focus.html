Pollen in focus

Adenanthos Meisneri

(Low - 50µm)

There are 33 species of Adenanthos, of which 31 are endemic to Western Australia. That is, 31 species are found naturally only in Western Australia.

(Medium - 25µm)

The triangular grains of Adenanthos meisneri have straight sides. This distinguishes them from Adenanthos obovatus which has pollen grains with concave sides.

(High - 10µm)

The cell wall of this pollen is thin. Pollen grains can be carried by the wind or transported by animals such as insects, birds or small mammals.

(Surface - 5µm)

These pollen grains have a moderately reticulate surface sculpture. That is, the lumina, which are spaces enclosed by ridges (muri), are greater than 1 µm in diameter. The ridges or muri of A. meisneri are thicker than those of A. obovatus.

Adenanthos Obovatus

(Low - 50µm)

Adenanthos obovatus pollen grains have three pores (triporate) and concave sides. The common name for this shrub is the basket flower. It grows to about 1.5 m and is found throughout south-west Western Australia.

(Medium - 25µm)

Adenantos obovatus pollen grains are triporate with concave sides.

(High - 10µm)

The diameter of a triangular pollen grain is measured from the middle of one pore to the centre of the opposite cell wall.

(Surface - 5µm)

A net-like pattern is clearly visible on the surface of this grain. Adenanthos obovatus has a moderately reticulate surface sculpture. That is, the lumina, spaces enclosed by ridges (muri), are greater than 1 µm in diameter.

Banksia Grandis

(Low - 50µm)

Banksia grandis is commonly known as bull banksia. Pollen grains are ovoid, biporate with convex sides.

(Medium - 25µm)

Banksia grandis pollen grains have a relatively thin cell wall. In spring, the amount of pollen in the air increases. This can cause allergies. A daily pollen count of 30/105 means there were 30 grass pollen and 105 total pollen grains per cubic metre of air, in the preceding 24 hours.

(High - 10µm)

The thin cell wall of these pollen grains is clearly visible. The outer wall of a pollen grain, called the exine, is made from sporopollenin. Fossilised pollen grains are found as sporopollenin, a very tough material that is extremely resistant to breaking down.

(Surface - 5µm)

Banksia grandis pollen grains have a very finely reticulate surface sculpture. That is, the lumina (holes on the surface) look like small dots.

Banksia Ilicifolia

(Low - 50µm)

Banksia ilicifolia is commonly known as the holly-leaved banksia. It is found in the south-west of Western Australia. Western Australian shrubby woodlands, where Banksia species are found, are known as kwongan heath.

(Medium - 25µm)

The pollen grains are ovoid and biporate. The pollen from all Banksia species have a net-like surface. Pollen grains contain the male sex cell (gametophyte) and germinate through their pores.

(High - 10µm)

The pollen from Banksia ilicifolia has a thicker cell wall than Banksia grandis.

(Surface - 5µm)

Banksia ilicifolia pollen grains have a finely reticulate surface sculpture. That is, the ridges (muri) and spaces (lumina) are obvious, but less than 1 µm in diameter.

Banksia Littoralis

(Low - 50µm)

Banksia littoralis is commonly known as swamp banksia as it is found growing in low-lying swampy land, often near watercourses. As with many Banksia species it is endemic to the south-west of Western Australia. Seven pollen grains are visible on this slide.

(Medium - 25µm)

One of the pollen grains looks different from other grains in the slide. This is because Banksia littoralis pollen often folds along its width.

(High - 10µm)

Pollen grains vary in shape from spherical to elliptic to triangular. Banksia pollen is ovoid and biporate with convex sides. Of the 79 or more genera in the family Proteaceae, only Banksia and Dryandra are biporate (the others are triporate).

(Surface - 5µm)

Banksia littoralis pollen grains have a very finely reticulate surface sculpture. That is, the outer surface of the pollen grain has a net-like pattern where the lumina (holes) look like small dots.

Banksia Meisneri

(Low - 50µm)

Banksia meisneri is commonly known as Meisner's banksia. It is named after the 19th century Swiss botanist, Carl Meissner (his family name was Meisner but he later changed its spelling). It is only found in south-west Western Australia.

(Medium - 25µm)

The pollen grains are ovoid, biporate and have convex sides. Banksia meisneri grows in sandy soils. It flowers from April through to September.

(High - 10µm)

Banksia meisneri has a smaller aperture than many other Banksia species. The width and length of a pollen grain are measured outside the pollen wall.

(Surface - 5µm)

Banksia meisneri pollen grains have a finely reticulate surface sculpture with obvious spaces (lumina) and ridges (muri).

Banksia Occidentalis

(Low - 50µm)

Banksia occidentalis is commonly known as the red swamp banksia. It is only found along the southern coast of Western Australia.

(Medium - 25µm)

The pollen grains are ovoid in shape and biporate. Banksia occidentalis flowers in spring, summer and autumn. Unfortunately, it is susceptible to dieback. This plant disease, caused by the fungus Phytophthora cinnamomi, causes root rot.

(High - 10µm)

Banksia meisneri has pollen grains that are small compared with those of other Banksia species. The cell wall of the pollen grain is clearly visible.

(Surface - 5µm)

Banksia occidentalis pollen grains have a very finely reticulate surface sculpture. That is, the outer surface of the pollen grain has a net-like pattern where the spaces (lumina) look like small dots.

Grevillea Robyn Gordon

(Low - 50µm)

Grevillea species are found throughout Australia. They are often pollinated by small animals. In this slide five triangular pollen grains are visible.

(Medium - 25µm)

The pollen grains of Grevillea Robyn Gordon have straight sides with outer walls that look as if they are fringed. The fringing is called a flange. The flange is not included in the measurement of the pollen grain diameter or the wall width, as it is so irregular.

(High - 10µm)

The irregular flange is not included in the measurement of the pollen grain diameter or the wall width, as it is so irregular. Grevillea Robyn Gordon pollen grains are among the biggest in this pollen library.

(Surface - 5µm)

Grevillea Robyn Gordon pollen grains have a verrucate surface sculpture. That is, the surface is covered with wart-like bumps.

Hakea Lissocarpha

(Low - 50µm)

Hakea lissocarpha is commonly known as honeybush. It is endemic to Western Australia, where it is found from north of Geraldton through to Esperance.

(Medium - 25µm)

Hakea lissocarpha pollen grains are triangular with concave walls and a thick cell wall.

(High - 10µm)

The Hakea pollen grain in this slide has an intact aperture membrane on all three pores. The function of the membrane, or little cap over a pore, may be to protect the grain from micro-organisms or dehydration.

(Surface - 5µm)

The surface sculpture of Hakea lissocarpha pollen is known as verrucate. That is, the surface is covered in little lumps that are broader than they are high.
