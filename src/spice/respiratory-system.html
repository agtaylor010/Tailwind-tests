Respiratory system

Trachea

(Low - 250µm)

The large, grey area is part of the lumen (space within a tube) of the trachea. The cartilage ring that surrounds the lumen of the trachea is seen as a solid mass. Even at this low magnification, you cannot see both sides of the trachea at once. Epithelial cells can be seen on the inside edge of the trachea, but details are not visible.

(Medium - 50µm)

The epithelial lining of the trachea is visible here, as is the cartilage. The dark granules within the cartilage are chondrocytes (you will find them easier to see at high magnification).

(High - 50µm)

The ciliated epithelial lining can be seen towards the right of the image. Between this tissue and the cartilage rings are glands that moisten the surface of the trachea. The chondrocytes are readily visible, contained within the lacunae (small, hollow spaces).

Lung (cross section)

(Low - 250µm)

This view shows the alveoli, ducts and a few blood vessels within the lung. The 'jigsaw' appearance is due to alveolar sacs and individual alveoli. Alveolar ducts of different sizes are scattered throughout this image. Towards the centre are two blood vessels.

(Medium - 50µm)

The alveoli and blood vessels in the lung are fairly distinct at medium magnification. The blood vessels that can be seen in this image are surrounded by smooth muscle that can be seen more clearly by switching off the labels.

(High - 50µm)

This close-up concentrates on the alveoli. The alveolar walls are made from squamous epithelial cells. Associated with each alveolus is a capillary. These are so thin that it is impossible to differentiate between the alveoli and capillary walls.
