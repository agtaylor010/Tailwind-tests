Satellite: Hubble Space Telescope

The Hubble Space Telescope (HST) is a space telescope that was carried into orbit by a space shuttle in April 1990. The telescope is expected to function until at least 2014, when its successor, the James Web Telescope is due to be launched.

Satellite: GPS

Global Positioning System (GPS) was developed by the US Department of Defense to provide all-weather, round-the-clock navigation for military ground, sea and air forces. It is now used in many civilian applications and industries around the globe. GPS employs 24 spacecraft in circular orbits inclined at 53.6 degrees.

Double Star 2 is a joint European-Chinese research satellite that was launched in 2004. Its elliptical orbit varies from 700Km to 39,000Km above the Earth. It is used to study magnetic processes around the Earth together with its companion, Double Star 1, which is in equatorial orbit.

Satellite: INTEGRAL

INTEGRAL, or International Gamme-Ray Astrophysics Laboratory, observes the most energetic radiation that comes from space. The spacecraft was launched in 2002 by the European Space Agency. Gamma-rays from astronomical sources can only be detected by satellites because the Earth's atmosphere acts as a shield to protect us from this cosmic radiation.

NOTE: The animations are based on the JTrack-3D database from NASA, which contains data on over 900 satellites. Not all of these satellites are operational. The Space Surveillance Network currently tracks more than 8000 objects in orbit. Around 560 of these are operational satellites - the rest are debris. All objects more than 10cm in size are tracked. The data is used to predict when and where a decaying space object will re-enter the Earth's atmosphere, prevent a returning space object from triggering missile-attacl warning sensors, and inform NASA when objects may interfere with the space shuttle and Russian MIR space station orbits.
