Tissue types

Ciliated epithelial tissue

(Low - 250µm)

The ciliated cells are seen in two rows, joined together by connective tissue. They appear to form a thin, dark-coloured line. At low magnification, the cilia are indistinct.

(Medium - 100µm)

At medium magnification, the detail of the structures becomes visible. The shape of the epithelial cells is easier to see, and you can see cilia in some places.

(High - 50µm)

At high magnification, much of the detail is revealed. The cilia are quite distinct; occurring in clumps protruding from some cells.

Squamous epithelial tissue

(Low - 250µm)

The cytoplasm of these cells is lightly stained purple while the nuclei are small black dots.

(Medium - 50µm)

At medium magnification, the cells are easier to see. They are oval in shape and the nuclei tend to be centrally located in each cell.

(High - 50µm)

The plasma membrane surrounds the cell. It controls the movement of substances into and out of the cell. Another membrane encircles the nucleus.

Skin tissue

(Low - 250µm)

The skin has two layers (the epidermis and the dermis). Below the dermis lies the subcutaneous region. Different layers can be seen within the epidermis, but they are clearer at higher magnifications. The dermis is composed of dense fibro-elastic material and is where hair follicles and sweat glands are located.

(Medium - 50µm)

Five layers can be seen in the epidermis. The large number of individual cells visible in the stratum basale indicates a high rate of cell division. Cells are constantly needed to replace the dead cells of the outer layer, the stratum corneum. The stratum corneum is between 15 and 20 cell layers thick.

(High - 50µm)

Cells of the stratum basale are quite distinct in this image. They are columnar or cuboidal in shape while cells in the more outer layers are flattened. Mitosis (cell division) does not occur in the outer three layers.

Adipose tissue

(Low - 250µm)

Adipose tissue can be seen to be a form of loose, connective tissue. Individual adipose cells (called adipocytes) can be seen, but not in detail.

(Medium - 50µm)

At medium magnification, the adipocytes, which make up adipose tissue, become distinct. The adipocytes appear to be large, empty cells. This is caused by a droplet of fat (lipid) inside the cell pushing the cytoplasm and nucleus to one side. In some cells, you can see the nucleus against the cell membrane.

(High - 50µm)

Enlarging the image to high magnification does not provide any additional information about the adipocytes.

Areolar tissue

(Low - 250µm)

This image reveals some of the structure of areolar tissue. A large number of collagen fibres are present. Areolar tissue also contains elastic fibres, but these cannot be seen at this magnification.

(Medium - 50µm)

At medium magnification, the collagen fibres do not appear to be as densely packed as they do when viewed at low magnification. This is indicative of areolar tissue, which is sometimes called loose connective tissue.

(High - 50µm)

At high magnification elastic fibres are visible within the areolar tissue. The elastic and collagen fibres are seen to be in a semi-fluid matrix, an arrangement that provides strength, elasticity and support.
